Wildfire Modeling in the Wildland-Urban Interface

I. Introduction

In October of 1991, a firestorm tore through the hills of Berkeley and Oakland, CA, leaving 1,800 homes, 2000 vehicles and 14 people in its wake (1).   The event was the worst fire in CA history and provided a harsh reminder of the risk of fire in Mediterranean climates, especially in the Wildland Urban Interface (WUI).  The Oakland fire highlighted the dangers of fire suppression and a history of fire mitigation that ignored the natural processes of Mediterranean ecosystems.  Whether this involved the introduction of invasive species, the construction of dense housing developments, or the inability to conduct large scale controlled burns, the legacy of these outcomes have proven time and time again that these fire suppression strategies are a disaster waiting to happen (2).  There is a clear need for alternative methods to mitigate the risk of fire transmission in the WUI and ensure the creation of safer and more sustainable communities in Mediterranean climates.  

The first step to solving this problem is by gaining a better understanding of fire behavior in these spaces.   Fire modeling has been the foundation for fire mitigation since Dick Rothermel developed the first easily replicable fire model in 1972.  His methodology was developed in the context of wildfires but his understanding of the relationship between fire, fuel types, wind speed, and slope have remained universally applicable (3).  In the context of the WUI, there are increasingly challenging factors that need to be taken into account, namely the built environment and its relationship to surrounding vegetation.  The acquisition of discrete data to model these factors is critical to a model’s success. Once a high fidelity model is created, it is possible to provide localized, prescriptive changes to the form, structure, and composition of fuels in an area. In light of this, our research aims to address the unique challenges of the WUI and provide tangible solutions by utilizing high resolution, multispectral imagery, Airborne Lidar, vector-based fire modelling software, community driven data collection, and site specific fuel modeling to inform comprehensive fire mitigation strategies for communities in California and France with universal application to Mediterranean climates around the world.

II. Calibrating Our Fuel Model

In the context of fire modeling, the WUI can be represented as a contiguous surface of patches of homogenous fuel types, each patch having different fuel characteristics from its surrounding patches.  The structure and composition of these fuel patches are critical variables that contribute to how wildfires propagate in the WUI. Constructing our model will be a process of adjusting parameters in a segment mean shift (SMS) algorithm and iterating through potential fuel models until we generate a high fidelity representation of the fuel patch structure and composition in the study area.  Once properly calibrated to the WUI in Mediterranean climates, an SMS model excels in its speed and potential for scalability.  However, creating an SMS model requires a means of accuracy assessment for appropriate parameterization.  We turn to crowdsourcing and stakeholder engagement to calibrate our fuel model to the Orinda Downs neighborhood in Northern California and the XX neighborhood in some part of France. III. Mitigating Risk in the WUI

              The final model deliverable will use FARSITE and FlamMap spatial wildfire modeling software to create wildfire spread rate and intensity simulations.  The simulations will integrate the SMS image analysis, the crowdsourced discrete fuel data, and topographical and weather data to create spatial indexes of risk within the study site. Potential mitigation practices will be evaluated by amending the fuel inputs to the simulation to maximize effective firebreaks and defensible space at minimal cost and intrusion.  These mitigation practices may include canopy thinning, ladder removal, creating defensible buffers around buildings, among other procedures to counteract the effects of historical suppression. To maximize the use-value and impact of our work, the results of the risk analysis and mitigation option evaluations will be presented directly to the homeowner via homeowners associations and direct engagement.  Our project aims to establish a precedent of agency among homeowners to abate fire risk around their own homes.  Fire protection in the United States is blighted by the culture of the public good, such that local government is the primary agent responsible for risk abatement and abatement is naturally underprovided.  Our project offers state of the art fire risk consulting directly to the homeowner to overcome capital constraints that impede the paradigm shift of infrastructure fire risk management to one of homeowner responsibility.

IV. Collaborative Research

Fire risk is not an isolated issue and the potential risk to the lives and the properties of urban fringe communities is devastating.  These realities are especially true in Mediterranean climates, and tackling these challenges will require a collaborative approach that emanates best practices and ensures the accessibility of leading research.  It is our goal to further this approach through a Californian and French research collaborative that will enable us to share knowledge, build flexibility in our modeling to account for the unique characteristics of our communities, and ultimately build an internationally defensible methodology for WUI fire modeling, data collection, and homeowner driven fire mitigation.
